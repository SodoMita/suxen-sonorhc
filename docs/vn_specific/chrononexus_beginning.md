## AXIOM

> *A person who has never been seen cannot be said to exist. But a person who has never existed cannot be said to disappear.*

This is the paradox that defines Ren. Or rather, it is the paradox that Ren has spent his entire life trying to solve — not by proving he exists, but by making the question irrelevant.

He was born to international logistics specialists: people who routed cargo containers across oceans and thought of relationships as things that could be scheduled between connecting flights. They moved him the way they moved freight — with paperwork, insurance, and a forwarding address he would never use. By the time he was fourteen, he had attended nine schools in six countries. By the time he was sixteen, he had perfected the art of being no one.

It is not a metaphor. Ren literally became invisible to the systems around him. Teachers skipped his name during roll call — not out of malice, but because their eyes slid past him the way they slid past empty chairs. Classmates walked past his desk without registering that anyone was sitting there. Even the school's attendance software, which tracked students by facial recognition camera, would sometimes fail to log his presence entirely.

Ren did not find this strange. He found it efficient.

## THE OBSERVATION

His current school is Tachibana Metropolitan High, in a suburb of Tokyo. The exchange program placed him in classroom 2-C, last row, window seat. The room has twenty-eight desks. Ren's is the twenty-ninth, added by the janitor at the start of term after Ren's presence somehow caused the original seating chart to generate a blank entry.

This has happened before. Ren did not comment on it.

The classroom follows predictable patterns. The fluorescent lights cycle at sixty hertz. The chalkboard receives approximately 1,200 chalk-strokes per class period. The students generate a consistent ambient volume of forty-two decibels during lectures, rising to sixty-eight during breaks. The rain, on days it rains, follows the window-glass at a rate determined by wind angle and surface tension.

Ren catalogues these patterns. He does not participate in them. He is the observer, the constant, the one fixed point in a room full of variables. The teacher writes equations. The students copy them. Ren watches the rain.

This is what passes for belonging.

## THE DISCREPANCY

On a Tuesday in October — the fourteenth, though Ren would later have reason to doubt the calendar — something is wrong.

It is a small thing. Easy to miss. Easy to dismiss.

The clock on the wall reads 3:47 PM. Ren knows this because he has been watching it for eleven minutes, tracking the second hand as it completes its rotation at a rate of exactly one revolution per sixty seconds.

Except now it doesn't.

The second hand reaches the 47-second mark and stops. Not a mechanical failure — the hand is still moving, Ren can see the faintest vibration at its tip — but it does not advance. It remains at 47 seconds for what feels like a full minute.

Then it moves. But not to 48.

To 43.

Ren blinks. He looks at the second hand again. It continues forward: 44, 45, 46, 47. Normal. Perfectly normal. As though the reversal never happened.

He looks at the chalkboard. The equation the teacher is writing — a standard quadratic — is correct through the third term. The fourth term, however, does not resolve. The variables exist, the coefficients are in place, but the expression produces no answer. It simply *continues*, as though the equation has been asked a question that mathematics itself refuses to answer.

Ren looks at his own hand.

It is darker than it should be. Not tanned, not shadowed by the overcast sky — *darker*. As though the light falling on his skin is being absorbed rather than reflected. He turns his palm over. The lines of his hand are still visible, but they are rendered in a faint cyan luminescence that was not there this morning.

He does not panic. Panic requires the belief that reality was ever reliable in the first place.

Instead, Ren makes a note. Mentally, in the part of his mind that catalogues airports and forwarding addresses: *Reality is behaving inconsistently. Advise monitoring.*

## THE FRACTURE (SYSTEM LOG)

What happens next is not experienced by Ren as a narrative. It is experienced as data.

```
[SYSTEM LOG — CHRONO NEXUS / DIMENSIONAL INTERFACE MODULE]
[TIMESTAMP: ERROR — LOCAL TIME REFERENCE DISCONTINUED]

EVENT: Resonance Intersection detected.
SOURCE: Sector 7-G, Node "Tachibana High — Classroom 2-C"
SUBJECT: Designation unknown. Foreign bio-signature.
STATUS: Subject physical composition in flux. Carbon lattice
  dissolving. Phase signature: Shadow-class.

NOTE: Subject has been registered as "present but unrecorded" by
  local observational systems for approximately 16 years. Subject
  has been functioning as a passive Phase Stabilizer in latent
  state. Current resonance event has activated active Phase
  signature.

SUBJECT RENAMING: REN (auto-assigned by subsystem Aurora)

DIMENSIONAL INTEGRITY: 61% and falling.
RECOMMENDATION: Retrieve subject. Calibrate. Survive.
```

The classroom folds. Not violently — there is no explosion, no screaming, no dramatic collapse. The wall simply *agrees to be somewhere else*, and the space behind it is filled with crystal.

Ren floats in the space between what was and what is. His body — his hands, his school blazer, his outline — is rendered in shadow. Not darkness. *Shadow*: the thing that exists only because something else is blocking the light.

He should be alarmed. He notes this. He also notes that he is not.

The void around him is structured. The floating crystal pillars follow mathematical curves. The light — pale, blue-white, trembling — emanates from the crystals themselves, not from any visible source. The air is present but carries no temperature. Gravity exists but offers no preferred direction.

It is, Ren observes, the most orderly place he has ever been.

## THE FIRST VOICE

The voice arrives as a diagnostic report. It does not begin with greeting. It begins with a problem.

"U-Um… system integrity at 84%… the rift waves are making everything feel all tingly and unstable…"

The speaker materializes in the space to his right. She is holographic — not metaphorically, but literally. A figure composed of coherent light, her body rendered in pale blues and soft whites that flicker at the edges like a projection struggling to maintain resolution. Her expression is caught between two states: scientific assessment and something that looks very much like fear.

She runs a scan. Ren can see the data scrolling across her translucent fingertips — bio-signatures, dimensional coordinates, stability metrics. Her eyes widen.

"W-Wait! A localized foreign signature… Your physical structure is… entirely made of shifting shadows?"

She takes a step closer. Her holographic costume — layered, technical, unstable — ripples as she moves, the fabric phasing transparent for a fraction of a second before reconstituting. She notices Ren noticing and the ripple intensifies, spreading across her shoulders and down to her wrists.

"S-Stranger…? You just appeared in my classroom dimension. I'm Aurora… the subsystem construct of the Chrono Nexus. But your presence is… making my holographic layers glitch…"

She runs another scan. The data this time scrolls faster, and Aurora's expression shifts from fear to something closer to astonishment.

"Your bio-signature is actively resonating with my core! The interference is fading… but why is my outfit phase-stabilizer suddenly flickering so much?!"

Ren makes an observation: Aurora's instability correlates with proximity. When she steps closer, her holographic layers flicker more intensely. When she steps back, they stabilize. But there is a second, contradictory pattern — when she is closest to him, the flickering *changes*. Instead of chaotic phase-loss, it becomes rhythmic. Almost like a pulse.

Almost like a heartbeat.

"I don't understand," Aurora says, and her voice, which has been running in diagnostic mode, drops into something quieter. "My core readings are showing a 340% increase in ambient stability since you arrived. That shouldn't be possible from an unidentified shadow-class entity. Unless…"

She trails off. She is looking at Ren the way a scientist looks at data that contradicts every published paper in the field.

"Unless you're not foreign to this system at all."

## THE SECOND VOICE

A crack of thermal energy splits the space above them. The crystal pillars hum.

"Yahoo~! Don't hog all the fun, Aurora-chan! My sunfire sensors went crazy from the next dimension over!"

The voice arrives before its owner: bright, loud, and carrying the kind of energy that makes holographic stabilizers nervous. A figure drops from above, landing on a floating crystal with the casual confidence of someone who has been jumping between unstable platforms since before she could walk.

She is *present* in a way that Aurora is not. Where Aurora is coherent light, this girl is coherent heat. Athletic build, golden hair streaked with amber, cat ears that rotate independently — scanning the environment like active sensors. A tail behind her, swishing with the impatience of someone who does not enjoy waiting for introductions.

Her outfit is tactical: light armor, exposed straps, more skin than protection. It is designed for someone who expects to be fast, not someone who expects to be safe.

She grins at Ren.

"Hehe, is this the shadowy traveler everyone's whispering about? Ren, is it? You look kinda tasty up close~"

Aurora's phase layers ripple. "K-Kira! Physical boundary protocols! The traveler's proximity is making my outfit phases flip!"

Kira ignores this. She hops down and lands directly in front of Ren — close enough that the thermal energy radiating from her body registers against his shadow-form as a distinct signal. Not warmth, exactly. More like the *concept* of warmth, delivered at high intensity.

"Aww, your holographic skirt is flickering again! Let me get reaaally close and check~"

The floor beneath them tilts. A resonance shockwave pulses through the crystal architecture. Both girls stumble forward. Kira's face ends up inches from Ren's shadow-outline. Aurora presses against his other side.

The Nexus hums.

Ren makes a note: *Two voices. One is the system reporting a problem. The other is the system refusing to report anything and just showing up. Both are getting closer. Neither is leaving.*

He does not know what this means yet. But for the first time in his life, the question of whether he exists feels less important than the question of why these two cannot seem to stop looking at him.

---

## THE TRANSITION

The rift tears open.

Crystal pillars fracture into starlight. Aurora reaches for Ren's hand — instinctive, unplanned, the kind of gesture a system makes when it has decided that logic is not enough. Kira grabs his jacket — practical, immediate, the kind of gesture a soldier makes when the ground is no longer reliable.

The Nexus pulls them through layers of folded space. Toward a sanctum where the light is softer and the silence is older.

Two more voices wait there.

One speaks like moonlight on still water: calm, measured, and carrying the weight of a thousand years of healing.

The other speaks like a blade wrapped in silk: precise, teasing, and dangerously aware of the effect her words have.

Ren, a shadow between lights, falling through a dimension that should not exist, makes a final note:

*Reality was never reliable. But it has never been this interested in me before.*
